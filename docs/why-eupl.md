# Why the European Union Public Licence

This project is licensed under the EUPL-1.2. If you are weighing it against
US-origin licences like MIT or Apache-2.0, here is why the European licence can
matter, especially for an organisation that operates under European law. This is
context, not legal advice. Check your own situation with counsel.

## It is written for European law

The EUPL names its legal framework explicitly:

- **Applicable law (Article 15)**: the licence is governed by the law of the
  European Union Member State where the licensor has its residence or registered
  office. There is a defined default when that does not apply.
- **Jurisdiction (Article 14)**: disputes go to the competent court where the
  licensor resides, or to the Court of Justice of the European Union when an EU
  institution is the licensor.

MIT and Apache-2.0 name **no governing law and no jurisdiction**. For a European
party that means the applicable law is left to conflict-of-laws rules and is less
predictable. The EUPL removes that uncertainty by design.

## It is valid in your language

Under Article 13, every official EU language version approved by the European
Commission has **identical legal value**, and you may rely on the version in your
own language. MIT and Apache-2.0 have a single canonical English text. A
translation carries no legal weight. For a business that does not work in English,
relying on a text in its own language, with full legal force, is a real
difference.

## It was drafted by the European Commission

The EUPL was created by the European Commission so public administrations and
others could share software under a licence aligned with **European copyright law
and terminology**, and it is recommended for public-sector reuse in the EU. Its
concepts map to EU directives rather than to a US common-law framing.

## It keeps adaptations open, with a compatibility bridge

The EUPL is a **copyleft** licence: derivatives stay under the EUPL or a listed
compatible licence (including the GPL and AGPL families). Improvements made on top
of it remain available. MIT and Apache-2.0 are permissive, which also allows
closed forks. Which you prefer depends on whether you want shared improvements or
maximum permissive adoption.

## When a US-origin licence still fits

If you want the widest possible permissive adoption, no copyleft, and you are
comfortable with an English-only text under unspecified governing law, MIT or
Apache-2.0 remain excellent choices. This is a question of fit with your legal
context and your goals, not a question of quality. For a project that lives under
European law and values legal and data sovereignty, a licence drafted in and for
the EU legal order is a coherent default.
